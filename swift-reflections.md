**Week 2: 12/03/2026:** 
In Leonardo Flores’s article “Third Generation Electronic Literature,” he suggests that E-Lit has developed through three generations, each shaped by different technologies, cultural contexts, and author communities.
The first generation came about before 1995, during the early days of personal computing. These works were somewhat experimental and created by a relatively small group of writers and programmers working with limited digital tools. A lot of this was hypertext fiction focusing on non-linear structures. Because the internet was not yet widely accessible, many works were distributed on floppy disks and needed special software to run. 
The second began around 1995 with the rise of the World Wide Web. Web technologies such as HTML and JavaScript allowed writers to create more complex. Electronic literature from this period often incorporated animation, sound, images, and interactive interfaces, expanding beyond purely text-based hypertext. The internet also made distribution wider and easier than ever before. 
The third generation of begins around 2005 and continues today, and it is defined by the widespread use of mainstream digital platforms. Instead of building custom interfaces or standalone websites, writers now create literary works directly using social media platforms, mobile apps, and web services. This generation reflects a major shift: digital writing practices are no longer limited to specialised communities but are embedded within everyday digital culture. Because millions of people use these platforms every day, the potential creators and audiences of electronic literature have expanded dramatically.

So pretty much, Flores’ generational model shows how electronic literature has evolved with technological change. From niche experimental works distributed on disks, to multimedia web projects, and finally to platform-based writing embedded in everyday online culture, each generation reflects different relationships between literature, technology, and audience participation. This framework also highlights how electronic literature has expanded beyond traditional literary spaces into the broader ecosystem of digital communication and social media creativity.
